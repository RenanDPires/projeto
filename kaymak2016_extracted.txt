Comparison of Analytical Methods for Calculating
the AC Resistance and Leakage Inductance of
Medium-Frequency Transformers
Murat Kaymak, Zhan Shen and Rik W. De Doncker
E.ON Energy Reasearch Center, Institute for Power Generation and Storage Systems, RWTH Aachen University,
Mathiuestr. 10, 52074 Aachen, Germany, post pgs@eonerc.rwth-aachen.de
Abstract—In this paper, a detailed investigation and compar-
ison of different analytical expressions for the high-frequency
resistance of medium-frequency transformers is presented. The
comparison is made by 2D FEM simulations and experimental
tests with suitable transformers. The deviations between the FEM
and the various analytical calculations are presented explicitly via
error contour maps to characterize the formulas for different ge-
ometrical parameters of common transformers. The comparison
shows that the modiﬁed Dowell-based method features accurate
results. The same study is also done to get an accurate prediction
of the desired leakage inductance of transformers by modifying
the formula based on Dowell.
Index Terms—Winding loss, resistance factor, leakage induc-
tance
I. I NTRODUCTION
The accurate design of high-power dc-dc converters de-
pends on the achievable efﬁciency at a certain operation
frequency of the medium-frequency transformer utilized for
isolation purposes. Higher power density can be achieved
with higher operating frequency to reduce the weight and
volume of the magnetic components yielding lower costs. At
the same time, the higher loss density requires a detailed
loss estimation and thermal model to design an appropriate
thermal management system [1], [2]. To guarantee an optimal
design with given speciﬁcations and requirements, adjusted
optimization algorithms are executed. Numerical methods such
like ﬁnite-element simulation (FEM) provide precise results
for the power losses. However, for large-scale geometries at
small skin depth, high-resolution meshes increase simulation
time and make a fast optimization difﬁcult [3], [4]. Besides
the calculation of the expected core loss of the transformer,
the winding ac resistance Rac at higher operation frequency
and high harmonic content of the current waveform has
large impact on the design process. The high frequency (HF)
winding loss are generated by induced eddy currents composed
of orthogonal skin and proximity effect [5]. Furthermore, at
certain dc-dc converter applications [6] an exact calculation
of the required leakage inductance of the medium-frequency
transformer as a function of the frequency also by closed-
form formulas is needed. Many investigations about the HF
winding loss have contributed to derive analytical expressions
for the frequency dependency of the ac resistance factor
Rac and the leakage inductance Lac, which are the theoret-
ical base of subsequent research [7]–[18]. Worth to mention
are the preliminary works of Butterworth [7] , Bennet and
Larson [9], P. Dowell [10] and Ferreira [19]. Some of the
previous contributions are summarized in [20] and have been
often used in the design of high-frequency transformer and
inductors [2], [21]. These mathematical models are commonly
based on the usage of Maxwell equations and simpliﬁed
assumptions of one-dimensional magnetic ﬁeld in parallel
to the winding layers [10], [20]. The analytical expression
derived by Dowell with main focus on foil-type conductors
and by Ferreira concerning round-type conductors are indeed
one of the most used calculation methods to compute the ac
resistance of transformer and inductor windings with the help
of a closed-form expression [5], [10], [11], [22]. However, in
many design cases, transformers and inductors feature a two-
dimensional magnetic ﬁeld distribution limiting the validity of
the simpliﬁed model [23]. Nevertheless, there are no detailed
comparison and investigations of the various calculation meth-
ods according to their sensitivity and accuracy as a function of
the physical dimension and winding type of the transformer.
Especially the design of medium-frequency transformer at ele-
vated frequency, voltage and power faces huge challenges due
to the sensitivity of the winding ac resistance Rac according to
the used winding type, winding arrangement and high-voltage
insulation within the winding area of the transformer. Although
most of the analytical calculations use the assumption of
one-dimensional component of the magnetic ﬁeld intensity
depending on the frequency, local induced extra currents due
to the two-dimensional nature of the magnetic ﬁeld lead
to higher or even lower losses than expected, also referred
to as “2D-effects” [24]–[26]. While the speciﬁed analytical
expressions applies only for purely sinusoidal waveforms,
various researchers have complemented these methods for
current waveforms with high harmonic content by means of
Fourier analysis or other approaches [14], [21], [27]–[31]. In
general the geometrical structure of the transformer winding
area varies according to the transformer speciﬁcations with
the inserted winding type and insulation distances. Moreover,
the construction of different core-types and core conﬁgura-
tions inﬂuences the 2D magnetic ﬁeld distribution within the
windings leading to edge-effect and additional losses, which

curve-ﬁtting methods or look-up tables [33], [40], [41]. In the
majority of cases, Dowell’s formula or Ferreira’s formula are
used for the modiﬁcations. The result of these investigations
are complicated empirical expressions with heavy mathemati-
cal handling or semi-analytical closed-form formula restricted
to a deﬁned range of possible winding conﬁgurations [33],
[40], [41]. Furthermore, insufﬁcient corresponding information
is given with respect to the validity range and limits of the new
adapted or empirical formulas. Some investigations about the
error allocation between the well-known formula and 2D FEM
simulation have been performed by the sensitivity analysis of
geometrical parameters of the winding area to point out and
evaluate the inaccuracy of the one-dimensional mathematical
model [33], [42]–[44]. In this context, high inaccuracies of
the classical approaches of Dowell and Ferreira are reported
in literature [3], [17], [32], [41].
From this point of view, detailed ﬁnite-element analysis (FEA)
is a key tool to analyze and determine the impact of core
as well as the winding geometry. So in this paper, an in-
depth FEA investigation of HF loss in foil and round windings
is executed and compared to selected analytical calculation
methods. Thus, the impact of the core type, the operating
point of the transformer and the geometry parameters of the
winding area on the one-dimensional or modiﬁed analytical
calculations are analyzed in detail in relation to the 2D-
effects [17], [32]. Additionally, the goals of this paper are
as follows: 1) the detailed description and classiﬁcation of
the different calculation formulas and thereby an overview of
literature; 2) the study of different expressions in dependency
of the physical dimensions of the transformer; 3) the overall
comparison of the formulas between each other with the help
of 2D FEM as a function of the major geometrical parameters;
4) the evaluation of the most promising expression for the
winding ac resistance Rac and leakage inductance with exper-
imental results. With the aid of the error distribution between
the analytical calculations and the 2D FEM it is possible
to get deeper knowledge about the behavior of the formulas
dependent on the frequency, the geometrical parameters and
the winding type. Therefore, this study enables the qualitative
and quantitative evaluation of the used analytical expression
to the real results of the transformer and clariﬁes their validity
range and accuracy. For a certain transformer conﬁguration it
is then possible to adapt the formula by empirical coefﬁcients
to adjust to the FEM and real results.
Brieﬂy, some of the equations used in this paper are introduced
ﬁrst: a) the classical Dowell method (Dowell 1) [10]; b) the
classical modiﬁed Dowell method (Dowell 2); c) the fully
modiﬁed Dowell method by Ferreira (Ferreira 1) [12]; d)
The original Ferreira method (Ferreira 2) [5]. Here, similar
to Dowell’s method, it is also possible to modify Ferreira 2
by the area-equivalent method in the opposite way in order
to apply Ferreira 2 for foil and rectangular conductors; e) the
modiﬁed Ferreira method by Reatti (Reatti) [16]; e) the FEM-
modiﬁed Dowell method by Dimitrakakis (Dimitrakakis) [44].
Measurements to validate the FEM and analytical results are
performed by the precision impedance analyzer Agilent 4294A
with more than seven transformers.

IV. C ONCLUSIONS
The most important commonly used expressions to calculate
the high-frequency resistance Rac have been compared by
2D FEM simulations and measurements regarding medium-
voltage medium-frequency transformers. The impact of the
geometrical parameters of a typical transformer on the resis-
tance factor Fr and leakage inductance Lσ are studied and
the relevant parameters are ascertained. The sensitivity of the
analytical methods to the porosity factor, penetration depth
and number of layers are investigated in detail and Dowell
2 is identiﬁed as the most accurate expression. The error
contour maps are derived for design checking and for further
adjustment of the formulas. The frequency-dependent leakage
inductance calculation method is modiﬁed to achieve high
accuracy at low porosity factor of the winding.
